About Me

Who Am I?

I like everything, to the point of maybe having a problem. For a long time, this was to my detriment. I struggled in college because, in my inexperience, I didn't know how to blend things like academics and, say, motorcycles, so they both suffered. It wasn't until much later in life that I met people who carved their own careers around their interests—something I had no idea was even possible. These individuals helped me see the interdisciplinary worlds that exist at the intersections, and opened my eyes to the idea that a lack of a node doesn't mean a lack of possibility.

This website is intended to be professional, but will naturally wander into the personal. Software engineers in particular, I think, will be familiar with this setup, as their "outside" projects often end up being a large part of their portfolio. I'm simply expanding this idea beyond programming to include a broader overview of how I spend my time. Needless to say, I'm a big fan of organized chaos' best friend, focused unfocusness. I believe that one of the most impossible things to do is learn something useless—any new knowledge or perspective will come in handy eventually. This also helps keep me engaged in a number of projects at any given time. At the moment, I'm most excited by:

Research Assistant Stanford University

While I was in grad school, my advisor noted that most bioethicists have a biology degree, so I figured that first step into the bio world would be some labwork. I asked around and ended up as a RA at Stanford, helping a Ph.D. student with his dissertation.
The project concerned the relative encephalization of North American mammals—that is, looking not at overall brain size, but at brain size relative to body size. My work was divided into four main sections:

  • Measuring skull volume of mammals & recording data
  • Analyzing game-cam photos of animals for species identification
  • Helping track bugs and doing outreach for proprietary metadata-editing program (photospread)
  • Tracking & analyzing data via R; Excel
